Pricing Interest Rate Swaps


An interest rate swap is an agreement between two parties to exchange future interest rate payments over a set period of time. There are two legs associated with each party. Swaps are the most popular OTC derivatives that are generally used to manage exposure to fluctuations in interest rates.

You may find different swap valuation models online: some just for intuitive understanding, some obsolete and others not even correct. In this page, we elaborate the real-world model used in the market for calculating the fair value and risk of a swap. We are focusing on the following most heavily traded swap types covered by FinPricing

  • Vanilla Interest Rate Swap (See details)
  • Interest Rate Basis Swap (See details)
  • Interest Rate Amortizing Swap or Accreting Swap (See details)
  • Average Compounded Swap (See details)

  • One of the most important factors for pricing a swap, including valuing a fixed leg, is to generate accurate cash flows.   • The accrual period is calculated according to the start date and end date of a cash flow plus day count convention

Final practical notes

  • Interest rate swaps are the most popular OTC derivatives. Most of them are either collateralized or cleared in the market. Therefore, pricing model should use OIS discounting to account for collateralization.
  • Some dealers take bid-offer spreads into account. In this case, one should use bid curve constructed from bid quotes for forwarding and offer curve built from offer quotes for discounting.

4. Average Compounded Swap

An average compounded swap consists of two legs: a regular fixed leg and an average compounded floating leg. The average compounded leg is similar to a regular floating leg except the reset frequency is higher than the payment frequency. For example, an average compounded leg has 1 month reset frequency and 3 month payment frequency. The most popular average compounded swap is Overnight Indexed Swap (OIS).
